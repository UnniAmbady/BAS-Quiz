The primary function of temperature sensors in a Building Automation System (BAS) is to provide the real-time temperature data that the system's controller uses to automatically manage the heating, ventilation, and air-conditioning (HVAC) equipment.

In simpler terms, they are the "nerve endings" of the building, telling the BAS "brain" how hot or cold it is in various locations. This information is the critical input needed to maintain a comfortable and efficient indoor environment.

How it Works: The Control Loop

This primary function is part of a continuous process called a control loop:

Measure: A temperature sensor measures the current temperature of a specific space (e.g., an office, a hallway, or inside an air duct).

Communicate: The sensor sends this temperature reading as an electrical signal or digital data point to a BAS controller.

Compare: The controller compares the measured temperature to a pre-programmed setpoint (the desired temperature, e.g., 72°F / 22°C).

Decide: Based on the difference between the actual temperature and the setpoint, the controller's logic decides what action is needed.

If the room is too hot, it needs to cool.

If the room is too cold, it needs to heat.

If the room is at the setpoint, it does nothing.

Command: The controller sends a command to the appropriate HVAC equipment (like an air handler, a VAV box, or a chiller) to start, stop, or adjust its output to bring the temperature back to the setpoint.

This loop runs continuously, 24/7, making constant, small adjustments to maintain the desired conditions.

Secondary (but still critical) Functions

While HVAC control is the primary function, temperature sensors enable several other vital functions in a modern BAS:

Energy Efficiency: By preventing overheating and overcooling, sensors are fundamental to energy conservation. The BAS can use outdoor air temperature data to decide when to use "free cooling" (economizer mode) instead of running expensive air conditioning compressors.

Occupant Comfort and Health: Maintaining consistent and comfortable temperatures directly impacts the productivity, satisfaction, and well-being of the people inside the building.

Monitoring and Alarming: Sensors are used to monitor critical areas like server rooms, data centers, or medical storage. If the temperature goes outside of a safe range, the BAS can trigger an alarm to notify facility managers before equipment fails or assets are damaged.

Data Logging and Analytics: The BAS stores historical temperature data from all its sensors. This data is invaluable for troubleshooting problems, identifying long-term performance trends, and optimizing building operations for even greater efficiency.

Equipment Protection: Sensors on equipment like boilers and chillers monitor water temperatures to ensure they are operating within safe and efficient parameters, preventing damage from extreme conditions.

In summary, while a temperature sensor's job is simply to measure temperature, its primary function within a BAS is to be the foundational data source that enables automated, intelligent, and efficient control of a building's climate.
